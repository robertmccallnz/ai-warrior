*For The Kiwi Dialectic / AI Warrior social media kit — Port Chalmers, Dunedin*

## 1. Brief Biography

Hone Papita Raukura "Ralph" Hotere ONZ (11 August 1931 – 24 February 2013) was born in Taikarawa, a Māori settlement between the Hokianga and Whāngāpē Harbours in Northland, of **Te Aupōuri** (and Te Rarawa) descent — hapū rooted in Te Hiku o te Ika, the Far North. He trained at Dunedin's King Edward Technical College and the Central School of Arts and Crafts in London (1961 fellowship), before settling permanently in **Koputai / Port Chalmers** from 1969, drawn there by the Frances Hodgkins Fellowship at the University of Otago and, he said, by a city that "accept[ed] I am a painter and leave me to go about my work." For four decades Port Chalmers was the geographic and spiritual centre of his practice: his studio overlooked Otago Harbour toward Aramoana, and his garden at Careys Bay became the site of *Black Phoenix II* (1991). He died in Dunedin on 24 February 2013 and was buried at Hione Urupa, Mitimiti — returned to the whenua of Te Aupōuri. Sources: [Te Ara Dictionary of New Zealand Biography](https://teara.govt.nz/en/biographies/6h3/hotere-hone-papita-raukura-ralph/print); [Govett-Brewster Art Gallery collection note](https://govettbrewster.com/collection/68-2-5).

## 2. Signature Visual Vocabulary

### Black Window Series (1981–1990)
Reclaimed colonial villa sash window frames — glass removed, glazing replaced with blackened board — operate simultaneously as protest sign, nocturnal view, and spiritual portal. In *Black Window – Towards Aramoana* (1981), the board is painted in oil on hardboard, set within a found window frame with its original brass lock still attached; a white cross marks where the transom and mullion once divided the panes, and reversed stencil letters spell Koputai (Māori name for Port Chalmers, meaning "the stomach/belly of the sea"). The blackness offers no view outward — Hotere's refusal to let the viewer see Aramoana is the protest. Source: [Auckland Art Gallery Chartwell Collection](https://www.aucklandartgallery.com/explore/art-and-artists/artwork/12591/black-window-towards-aramoana); [Art+Object essay on Black Window series](https://artandobject.co.nz/news/ralph-hotere-black-window).

### Black Phoenix / Boat Wreck Works (1984–1988)
*Black Phoenix* (1984–88) is Hotere's largest and most iconic work: the charred hull and planks of the fishing boat *Poitrel*, which he watched burn at Miller and Tunnage boatbuilders in Careys Bay from his studio window, assembled into a 5m × 13m × 5.5m installation. Planks form a pathway to the prow; the floor components are incised with an angle-grinder bearing the whakataukī *Ka hinga atu he tetekura, ara mai he tetekura* ("When one fern frond falls, another rises to take its place"). The work deliberately evokes the ancestral pā of Ngāti Ruanui, set alight to enable escape under black smoke — and simultaneously invokes the phoenix myth, the Te Maori touring exhibition then showing in the United States, and the regenerative power of fire. Material: burnt wood and metal. Collection: Te Papa Tongarewa. Source: [Te Papa Collections Online](https://collections.tepapa.govt.nz/object/37093).

### Aramoana Works — Anti-Aluminium-Smelter Protest (late 1970s–1984)
When a foreign consortium proposed an aluminium-processing plant at Aramoana — the fragile sand-dune spit at the mouth of Otago Harbour, whose Māori name means "pathway to the sea" — Hotere mobilised his entire visual language against it. He painted on **corrugated iron**, nailed protest works to telephone poles, and produced over a dozen *Towards Aramoana – Black Window* compositions. The 1980–83 *Aramoana* (Auckland Art Gallery, Chartwell Collection: acrylic and enamel on corrugated iron and wood, 2460 × 860 mm) uses industrial substrate as ground — the corrugation itself referencing the kind of factory roofing the smelter would have required. Reversed text, sequential stencilled numerals (reading like a countdown), and bursts of "dark, fiery reds" are embedded in the black field. As the Chartwell catalogue noted, only in one quadrant of the black is there "stenciled [text] in reverse (that is, 'unnatural') and dark, fiery reds, suggestive of the smelter's fires in the night." The 'X' mark — Hotere's "all cancelling and all-refusing" deletion symbol — crosses out an affixed photocopy of the smelter's official sign. Sources: [Auckland Art Gallery *Aramoana* 1980–83](https://www.aucklandartgallery.com/explore/art-and-artists/artwork/12633/aramoana); [Art+Object — Lynda Tyler on *Towards Aramoana*](https://artandobject.co.nz/news/lynda-tyler-on-ralph-hoteres-towards-aromoana); [Art+Object — *Aramoana/Koputai*](https://artandobject.co.nz/news/ralph-hotere-aramoana-koputai).

### Polaris / Nuclear Protest Works (1962, 1983–1984)
First made in France in 1962 during the Cuban Missile Crisis — "heavily layered, textured, scratched and scraped" oil-and-mixed-media works on hardboard — the *Polaris* series was revived with new urgency in 1983–84 when nuclear-powered US ships visited New Zealand under ANZUS. For *1984 No. 9* (Christchurch Art Gallery), Hotere worked sheets of stainless steel (reportedly sourced from a Fisher & Paykel factory truck) with a buffer, grinder, and blowtorch to produce "ravaged surfaces that thrummed with violence." A missile form surges up a scorched paper sheet, countered by a ragged X. The highly reflective surface functions as mirror: the viewer sees themselves implicating themselves. The later *Polaris* (1989, private collection) uses burnished corrugated steel in a sash window frame, with lettering blowtorched directly into the metal and "violet-pink paint that sprays out from either side of the corrugated panel" like a mushroom cloud. Sources: [Christchurch Art Gallery *1984 No. 9*](https://christchurchartgallery.org.nz/collection/84-18/ralph-hotere/1984-no-9); [Dunedin Public Art Gallery DPAG *Ātete* Exhibition Labels PDF](https://dunedin.art.museum/assets/EX-HOTERE-ATETE/HOTERE-ATETE-LABELS.pdf).

### Numerical and Roman-Numeral Sequences (*Pathway to the Sea*, Sangro, Malady)
Numbers in Hotere's work carry elegiac and political weight simultaneously. In the *Sangro* series (1962–64), ages copied from Māori Battalion headstones are arranged into a Greek cross with an X crossing out the space beneath — grief made geometric. In the *Malady* series (1970–71), Roman numerals I–XV mark paintings sequenced like the Stations of the Cross, mapping Bill Manhire's concrete poem onto devotional structure. Stencilled numerals in the Aramoana works run in reverse — unreadable, like a countdown to an explosion that the artist is trying to prevent. In *Pathway to the Sea – Aramoana* (1993 installation with Bill Culbert), pāua shells and fluorescent light trace a numbered pathway — the 'ara' (line/path) given literal form. Source: [Christchurch Art Gallery *Sangro Litany*](https://christchurchartgallery.org.nz/collection/8129/ralph-hotere/sangro-litany); [DPAG Exhibition Labels PDF](https://dunedin.art.museum/assets/EX-HOTERE-ATETE/HOTERE-ATETE-LABELS.pdf).

### Crucifixion / Black Painting Series (1968–1969 and ongoing)
The seven-panel *Black Paintings* (1968, Govett-Brewster Art Gallery) are Brolite lacquer on hardboard; each panel holds a precisely painted pinstripe cross representing one colour of the spectrum — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet — barely visible against a field of deep black lacquer buffed to a shimmer. The cross in Hotere's work is never only Christian; it is also the X of cancellation, the compass cross of navigation, the telescopic reticle sighting a target. Off-centre, faint, or inscribed backward, these crosses encode irreducible ambiguity. Source: [Govett-Brewster *Black Painting 5: Blue on Black*](https://govettbrewster.com/collection/68-2-5); [Fletcher Trust Collection *Black Painting*](https://fletchercollection.org.nz/artworks/black-painting/).

### Use of Corrugated Iron, Lead, Found Industrial Material
From his first corrugated-iron Aramoana works in the early 1980s, Hotere made the substrate the message: the very roofing iron that might have covered the smelter becomes the painting. Later works like *Yellow Triptych* (2001–03, Te Papa) use zincalume (aluminium-zinc alloy sheet) and lead-headed nails — "materials ordinarily used in roofing — into a polished and poetic artwork" — referencing *arte povera*'s reinvention of the everyday. The *Polaris* works use stainless steel worked with grinders and blowtorches; the *Black Phoenix* uses burnt boat timber. No substrate is inert: each carries the industrial or ecological history the work protests against. Sources: [Te Papa *Yellow Triptych*](https://collections.tepapa.govt.nz/object/651085); [Fletcher Trust *Nineteen Eighty Four*](https://fletchercollection.org.nz/artworks/nineteen-eighty-four/); [Art AsiaPacific obituary](https://www.artasiapacific.com/news/ralph-hotere-new-zealand-artist-dies-at-81/).

### Text as Image: Hand-Scratched/Incised Lettering, Biblical Quotations, Collaborations, Te Reo Māori
Text is structurally central to Hotere's work from 1962 onward. Methods include: stencilling with industrial wool-bale stencils (Malady, Sangro); freehand brushwork over the surface (No Ordinary Sun, Requiem); blowtorch lettering directly into steel (Oputae series); angle-grinder inscription into wood (Black Phoenix proverb); pencil outlining (Te Whiti drawings). Collaborators whose words he absorbed include:
- **Hone Tuwhare** (Te Aupōuri / Ngā Puhi): *No Ordinary Sun*, *Rain*, *O Africa*, *We Who Live in Darkness*
- **Bill Manhire**: *Malady*, *Pine*, *Dawn/Water Poem*, *Song Cycle*
- **Cilla McQueen** (Hotere's wife 1973–1986): *Black Window* (series named from her poem "Out the Black Window"), *Song of Solomon*, *Return to Sangro*
- **Ian Wedde**: *Pathway to the Sea* (1975 poetry collection; Hotere's cover art led directly to the Aramoana series)

Words are never illustration; as Vincent O'Sullivan wrote, "the words became the painting." Text appears backwards, upside-down, doubled, counted, dissolved. Sources: [City Gallery Wellington *Out the Black Window* exhibition page](https://citygallery.org.nz/exhibitions/ralph-hotere-out-the-black-window/); [DPAG Exhibition Labels PDF](https://dunedin.art.museum/assets/EX-HOTERE-ATETE/HOTERE-ATETE-LABELS.pdf); [NZ History *Black Painting XV*](https://nzhistory.govt.nz/media/photo/black-painting-xv-ralph-hotere-0); [Hone Tuwhare Charitable Trust](https://honetuwhare.org.nz/hotere/).

### Drips, Scratches, Gestural Marks Through Black Lacquer
Early Black Paintings (1967–69) use Brolite lacquer — an automotive finishing product — buffed to a mirror-like sheen, into which hairline circles and crosses are painted with a ruling pen, "sitting proud on the sprayed lacquer black ground" (International Art Centre auction note). Later works, particularly the Aramoana series, involve thinned black and burnt-sienna acrylic scrubbed into surfaces in layers, creating "mysterious depth"; Hotere used four bricks to weight canvas down while scrubbing. The Black Window 1982–90 shows "vivid restless brushwork" — blazing red ground nearly obliterated by "black staccato brushstrokes that coil into an obscuring soot, a dense smoke that yields to an overriding silver patina." Source: [Art+Object *Black Window* essay](https://artandobject.co.nz/news/ralph-hotere-black-window); [Art+Object *Towards Aramoana*](https://artandobject.co.nz/news/lynda-tyler-on-ralph-hoteres-towards-aromoana).

### Tin, Zinc, Oil Drum, Weathered Metal Substrates
Hotere's industrial materials vocabulary: corrugated iron (early Aramoana, 1980s), corrugated stainless steel (Polaris, 1984–89), zincalume sheet (Yellow Triptych, 2001–03), lead-headed nails as decorative and structural element (Yellow Triptych), recycled sash window frames as containing structure (Black Window throughout), burnt boat timber (Black Phoenix), blowtorch-worked stainless steel (Oputae, Polaris). The material provenance matters: salvaged from demolition sites in Port Chalmers, repurposed factory steel, the actual burnt wreckage of a local fishing boat. The work does not represent industrial devastation — it *is* made from it. Sources: [Te Papa *Yellow Triptych*](https://collections.tepapa.govt.nz/object/651085); [DPAG Exhibition Labels PDF](https://dunedin.art.museum/assets/EX-HOTERE-ATETE/HOTERE-ATETE-LABELS.pdf).

### Hotere Red

The red in Hotere's protest works — Aramoana, Dawn/Water Poem, Black Window 1982–90, What's in a Game? — is not a primary red. Documented descriptions: "dark, fiery reds" (Chartwell/Pacific Parallels catalogue, Black Window); "a blazing red ground" nearly obliterated by black brushstrokes (Art+Object, Black Window 1982–90); "vivid red to suggest apocalypse, anger and sacrifice" (Christchurch Art Gallery, *Dawn/Water Poem*); "garish orange-red ground" in *What's in a Game?* (Art News Aotearoa). The paint type is acrylic or enamel. The *Dawn/Water Poem* series uses what the Fletcher Trust catalogue calls **"rusty red"** — not a clean cadmium red but a red with iron-oxide undertones, evoking both blood and corrosion.

## 4. Typography and Text Treatment

Hotere's relationship to text is structural, not decorative. Words are a form of mark-making on equal footing with line, colour, and substrate.

**Methods, from most controlled to most gestural:**

1. **Industrial stencil (wool-bale stencil)** — used for *Malady* panels (1970–71): Manhire's concrete-poem words *MALADY / MELODY / MY LADY* enlarged to near-monumental scale using the industrial stencils used for labelling wool bales. Letters are sans-serif block capitals, evenly spaced, repeated in columns. Sources: [DPAG Exhibition Labels](https://dunedin.art.museum/assets/EX-HOTERE-ATETE/HOTERE-ATETE-LABELS.pdf); [City Gallery *Out the Black Window*](https://citygallery.org.nz/exhibitions/ralph-hotere-out-the-black-window/).

2. **Reversed / mirror stencil lettering** — in Aramoana works, text is stencilled backwards, "making them read like a countdown to an explosion" (Lynda Tyler, Art+Object). The reversal makes letters legible as forms — shapes — while resisting easy reading: the viewer must consciously decode. Source: [Art+Object *Towards Aramoana*](https://artandobject.co.nz/news/lynda-tyler-on-ralph-hoteres-towards-aromoana).

3. **Freehand brushwork / cursive over-painting** — the poem text in *No Ordinary Sun* (1984) is "over-painted in a loose and cursive manner; the ivory-toned writing acts like clouds." In *What's in a Game?* (1988), text appears as "a combination of bold stencilled lettering and looping freehand scrawl … the improvisational aesthetics of protest materials." Source: [Auckland Art Gallery *No Ordinary Sun*](https://www.aucklandartgallery.com/explore/art-and-artists/artwork/20141/no-ordinary-sun); [Art News Aotearoa *What's in a Game?*](https://artnews.co.nz/ralph-hoteres-whats-in-a-game/).

4. **Blowtorch lettering into metal** — directly scorches text into corrugated stainless steel, as in the *Oputae* series (1989): "words on metal using a blowtorch." The result is oxidised, not painted — heat-drawn. Source: [Reed Gallery / Library of Ralph Hotere](https://www.reedgallery.co.nz/exhibitions/the-library-of-ralph-hotere/14); [DPAG Exhibition Labels](https://dunedin.art.museum/assets/EX-HOTERE-ATETE/HOTERE-ATETE-LABELS.pdf).

5. **Angle-grinder incision** — the *Black Phoenix* proverb is carved into wooden planks with an angle grinder. The cut is rough, physical, and indelible. Source: [Te Papa *Black Phoenix*](https://collections.tepapa.govt.nz/object/37093).

6. **Ruling-pen line on lacquer** — in early Black Paintings (late 1960s), fine colour circles and crosses are "applied with a ruling pen to sit proud on the sprayed lacquer black ground" — a draftsman's precision. Source: International Art Centre auction catalogue (Black Painting, July 2024).

**Text languages used:** English, te reo Māori, Latin (Requiem mass), French (*Le Pape est Mort*, *Mururoa*), Spanish (*Lo negro sobre lo oro*). Multiple languages sometimes appear in a single work.

**Key collaborating poets:**
- **Hone Tuwhare** (1922–2008, Ngā Puhi / Te Aupōuri) — *No Ordinary Sun*, *Rain*, *O Africa*, *We Who Live in Darkness*
- **Bill Manhire** (1946–) — *Malady*, *Pine*, *Dawn/Water Poem*, *Song Cycle*
- **Cilla McQueen** (1949–, Hotere's wife 1973–1986) — *Black Window* (series title), *Return to Sangro*, *Song of Solomon*
- **Ian Wedde** (1946–2017) — *Pathway to the Sea* (poetry collection; Hotere's cover led to the Aramoana corrugated-iron series)

Sources: [City Gallery Wellington *Out the Black Window*](https://citygallery.org.nz/exhibitions/ralph-hotere-out-the-black-window/); [Hone Tuwhare Trust](https://honetuwhare.org.nz/hotere/).

## 5. Compositional Grammar

Hotere's compositional language is reductivist but not emptied. The elements:

**Black field as ground:**  
Black is not background — it is the primary material and subject. The field is either void (matte, light-absorbing) or depth (lacquer, reflective). Against this ground, all other elements achieve maximum contrast and weight.

**Narrow vertical bar / column:**  
Hairline pinstripe verticals in the 1968 Black Paintings; the repeated columns of text (MALADY / MELODY / MY LADY) in the 1970–71 panels; the tall narrow format of *Sangro* panels. The vertical organises time: reading top to bottom is reading a sequence, a countdown, a litany.

**The horizon line:**  
In Aramoana works, a horizontal division evokes the actual view across the harbour — night water below, sky above. Often implied rather than stated, the horizon is the ecological threshold the smelter threatened to erase.

**Off-centre cross:**  
The cross appears: as barely visible pinstripe on lacquer; as a reversed or rotated form; as a Greek cross of numbers (Sangro Litany); as a blunt X of deletion. It is never centrally placed for symmetry's sake — its off-axis placement carries discomfort. In *Sangro Litany*, the cross of soldiers' ages is formed then "crossed out" by a large X beneath. The cross and the cancellation occupy the same composition.

**The X mark:**  
Distinct from the cross, the X functions as "all cancelling and all-refusing" (Francis Pound, cited in Christchurch Art Gallery). It appears over the smelter sign, over the Polaris missile form, over political events. In nautical terms it means "keep away."

**Text as field element:**  
Text does not sit in a designated text zone — it inhabits the same black void as the painted marks, sometimes obscuring and sometimes illuminating them. In *Malady* panels, the word IS the composition. In *Dawn/Water Poem*, "MURUROA" is written in vivid red across Manhire's repeated word "sunrise."

**Numbered sequence as composition:**  
Sequential numbers read as calendar, countdown, or litany. In Aramoana works, they run backward (reversed). In Sangro, they are ages of the dead, arranged as a cross. In Pathway to the Sea, the *ara* (path/line) is a numbered sequence through the gallery floor.

**Window frame as compositional boundary:**  
The sash window frame is not merely substrate — it divides the picture plane into quadrants (the cross implicit in the architecture), establishes a viewing relationship (inside/outside, viewer/viewed), and references the domestic architectural heritage of colonial New Zealand.

Sources: [Art+Object *Towards Aramoana*](https://artandobject.co.nz/news/lynda-tyler-on-ralph-hoteres-towards-aromoana); [Christchurch Art Gallery *Sangro Litany*](https://christchurchartgallery.org.nz/collection/8129/ralph-hotere/sangro-litany); [DPAG Exhibition Labels](https://dunedin.art.museum/assets/EX-HOTERE-ATETE/HOTERE-ATETE-LABELS.pdf); [Chartwell Trust *Pacific Parallels* catalogue note](https://chartwell.org.nz/seeing/exhibitions/pacific-parallels-artists-and-the-landscape-in-new-zealand/).

## 6. Te Reo Māori and Tiriti Context

Te reo Māori is not embellishment in Hotere's work — it is ancestral ground. His father Tangirau Hotere was a custodian of the Te Aupōuri waiata and a Catholic catechist who "imparted in his son a strong sense of language as an elemental presence like wind or rain" (DPAG Exhibition Labels). Text in te reo is often the most formally prominent element in the work.

**Key te reo inscriptions documented in specific works:**

- ***Godwit/Kuaka* (1977, mural / lacquer on hardboard):** The entire 18-metre mural is anchored by the Te Aupōuri *tauparapara* (chant) stencilled at its centre — the chant Hotere heard his father recite throughout his childhood, beginning *Ruia, ruia, opea opea, tahia tahia / Kia hemo ake / Ko te kaka koakoa...* ("Scattering, gathering, forming a single unit / Death/exhaustion rises up..."). The kuaka/godwit is a spiritual messenger for Te Aupōuri, linking the living and the dead across Te Moana-nui-a-Kiwa. Source: [Chartwell Trust *Godwit/Kuaka*](https://chartwell.org.nz/seeing/collection/godwitkuaka/).

- ***Black Phoenix* (1984–88):** Floor planks incised with angle-grinder: *Ka hinga atu he tetekura, ara mai he tetekura* ("When one fern frond falls, another rises to take its place"). Hotere adapts the standard wording slightly, changing *haramai* (pass over) to *ara mai* (the path forward) — the change corresponds to the physical pathway of bare wood leading to the charred prow. Source: [Te Papa *Black Phoenix*](https://collections.tepapa.govt.nz/object/37093).

- ***Te Whiti* series (c.1972):** These works on paper incorporate lament text associated with the sacking of Parihaka in 1881, drawn from a chant recorded by Te Rangi Hiroa: *E kore e pouri tonu Waitara I whakamamaetia i nga ra i mua ra…* ("Darkness will not always cover Waitara / That was caused to suffer pain in days gone by..."). As curator Greg O'Brien wrote: "words — with the messages and belief they contain — stand in for the lives of their authors: the political and spiritual leaders of the past, the ancestors." Source: [Christchurch Art Gallery *Parihaka Day* blog](https://christchurchartgallery.org.nz/blog/behind-the-scenes/2014/11/parihaka-day).

- ***Aramoana/Koputai* (1979, folding screen):** Te reo Māori place names for hills, islands and locations on and around Otago Harbour are written on six canvas panels in varying scripts and colours: *Mihiwaka, Mopanui, Rangiriri, Kamamautarua, Ngā Tamariki o Parewa, Te Ara o Te Makau, Tereweka.* These are not captions — they are acts of naming that reassert Māori geography against colonial renaming. Source: [Art+Object *Aramoana/Koputai*](https://artandobject.co.nz/news/ralph-hotere-aramoana-koputai).

- ***Drawing (KO WAI KOE?)* (pencil and stencil, Christchurch Art Gallery):** The stencilled Māori text *KO WAI KOE?* ("WHO ARE YOU?") melds with the abstract composition. A poem by Hotere's father is incorporated: *Tenei taku manu te rere atu nei — he karere — no Te whitinga mai o te ra. Pataia mai — i ahu mai Koe i hea? KO WAI KOE?* Source: [Christchurch Art Gallery blog](https://christchurchartgallery.org.nz/blog/collection/2007/03/drawing-ko-wai-koe-by-ralph-hotere).

- **He waka eke noa series (1972):** *He waka eke noa. It is a canoe which belongs to no one* — the title is also the inscription, asserting collective ownership of the political moment. Source: [DPAG Exhibition Labels](https://dunedin.art.museum/assets/EX-HOTERE-ATETE/HOTERE-ATETE-LABELS.pdf).

**Political and sovereignty context:**  
Hotere's use of te reo is inseparable from his politics. The *Black Painting* at Govett-Brewster is glossed in te reo and English, with the gallery noting that "black / mangu is associated with Ralph Hotere's works and evokes narratives of conflict, the loss of land and celestial beginnings" and connecting his palette to Māori creation whakapapa: Te Kore (the great void), Te Pō (the long night), and the separation of Ranginui and Papatūānuku. The Aramoana works resist an overseas corporation's claim on Māori whenua; the Parihaka works memorialise state violence against Māori sovereignty; the nuclear works connect French colonialism in the Pacific to nuclear testing. These are Tiriti works in that they assert the primacy of Māori relationship to land, sea, and language against colonial and corporate power. Sources: [Govett-Brewster](https://govettbrewster.com/collection/68-2-5); [Christchurch Art Gallery *Parihaka Day*](https://christchurchartgallery.org.nz/blog/behind-the-scenes/2014/11/parihaka-day).

## 7. Reference Works for the Designer

The following seven works are verified with working URLs the designer can access directly. Each offers a visual archetype from Hotere's practice.

### 1. *Black Phoenix* (1984–88)
**Medium:** Burnt wood and metal (assemblage)  
**Dimensions:** 5650 mm wide × 5000 mm high × 12900 mm long  
**Collection:** Museum of New Zealand Te Papa Tongarewa, Wellington (purchased 1988 with NZ Lottery Grants Board funds)  
**Description:** The regenerated hull and planks of the *Poitrel*, a fishing boat Hotere watched burn at Careys Bay from his Port Chalmers studio. The bow stands centrally; charred planks radiate as pathway and wall. Floor planks are incised with the whakataukī *Ka hinga atu he tetekura, ara mai he tetekura* using an angle grinder. The dominant material aesthetic: blackened, charred timber; dark metal; the incised mark. No paint as such — the black IS the burn. The connection to Te Aupōuri ancestors who escaped under cover of black smoke from a burning pā is built into the work's origin story.  
**URL:** [https://collections.tepapa.govt.nz/object/37093](https://collections.tepapa.govt.nz/object/37093)

### 2. *Black Window – Towards Aramoana* (1981)
**Medium:** Oil on hardboard in window frame  
**Dimensions:** 1095 × 907 × 55 mm  
**Collection:** Chartwell Collection, Auckland Art Gallery Toi o Tāmaki (C1994/1/78)  
**Description:** A reclaimed colonial villa sash window frame, glazing replaced with black-painted hardboard. A white cross marks the transom/mullion division. The upper-left quadrant shows reversed stencil letters — *Koputai* — and sequential numerals. One quadrant contains dark, fiery reds amid the black. The brass lock remains attached. The window offers no view to Aramoana — the blackening IS the protest.

### 3. *Aramoana* (1980–1983)
**Medium:** Acrylic and enamel on corrugated iron and wood  
**Dimensions:** 2460 × 860 mm  
**Collection:** Chartwell Collection, Auckland Art Gallery Toi o Tāmaki (C1994/1/133)  
**Description:** A tall, narrow vertical work on corrugated iron — the industrial substrate as protest material. The corrugation is both literal (roofing iron) and rhythmic (the harbour's surface, the smelter's infrastructure). Acrylic and enamel are applied directly over the iron, integrating the mark with the material's texture. The narrow vertical format echoes the Malady Panels and the harbour's own verticality (sky above, water below, spit between).

### 4. *No Ordinary Sun* (1984)
**Medium:** Enamel on board  
**Dimensions:** 1560 × 1180 mm  
**Collection:** Auckland Art Gallery Toi o Tāmaki (gift of Dame Jenny Gibbs, 2013; accession no. 2013/16)  
**Description:** A homage to Hone Tuwhare's famous poem — made on the poem's 21st anniversary. Palette: deep black and dark brown umber mixed with Van Dyke brown and Verdigris, over-burnished with a polisher. Tuwhare's poem text is over-painted in loose, cursive ivory-toned brushwork — "like clouds surrounding a darkened sun." The sun is occluded, as if by nuclear eclipse. One of the clearest examples of Hotere's method of absorbing poetry: the written words are both legible and dissolved into the painted field.

### 5. *Sangro Litany* (1979)
**Medium:** Acrylic on unstretched canvas  
**Dimensions:** 1809 × 915 mm  
**Collection:** Christchurch Art Gallery Te Puna o Waiwhetū (81/29; currently on loan to Te Rau Aroha museum, Waitangi)  
**Description:** A monochrome work in which ages of Māori Battalion soldiers (copied from headstones at the Sangro River War Cemetery by Hotere in 1962, including his brother Jack's) are arranged into a Greek cross, then crossed out by a large X. At the base, phrases from an ancient Māori godwit-return chant are stencilled. Paint runs and drips "like rain, blood or tears." The most explicit demonstration of Hotere's compositional grammar: number-as-cross, X-as-cancellation, stencil-text-as-litany, drip-as-grief.  
**URL:** [https://christchurchartgallery.org.nz/collection/8129/ralph-hotere/sangro-litany](https://christchurchartgallery.org.nz/collection/8129/ralph-hotere/sangro-litany)

### 6. *Dawn/Water Poem* (1986)
**Medium:** Acrylic on canvas  
**Dimensions:** 2430 × 1825 mm  
**Collection:** Christchurch Art Gallery Te Puna o Waiwhetū (86/50)  
**Description:** An anti-nuclear protest work responding to French testing at Mururoa. Hotere transforms Bill Manhire's poem into visual field: the word *MURUROA* is written in vivid red alongside Manhire's repeated "sunrise." In nautical terms, X means "keep away" — and Hotere uses it here. The red is acrylic, the background black, the text white/red against the dark field. One of the clearest examples of the red-on-black political register.  
**URL:** [https://christchurchartgallery.org.nz/collection/86-50/ralph-hotere/dawnwater-poem](https://christchurchartgallery.org.nz/collection/86-50/ralph-hotere/dawnwater-poem)

### 7. *Yellow Triptych* (2001–2003)
**Medium:** Paint on zincalume (aluminium-zinc alloy sheet) with lead-headed nails  
**Dimensions:** 2500 mm high × 2700 mm long (overall)  
**Collection:** Museum of New Zealand Te Papa Tongarewa  
**Description:** Three panels of zincalume — roofing material — with lead-headed nails as painterly and structural elements. Black, yellow, and the cross motif are brought together; the warm metallic ground of the zincalume provides the "gold" register. Hotere was "greatly influenced by *arte povera*, a movement emphasising the reinvention of everyday objects." The lead-headed nails create a textured, rhythmic surface across the metal. This work demonstrates the late palette: black on warm metallic ground, the gold/ochre of industrial material given the status of colour.

## 8. Designer-Ready Translation Brief

**Building in Hotere's aesthetic without copying any specific work:**

Work from a **black-dominant ground** — deep matte acrylic black as field, not background. Into this field, introduce a **single narrow vertical red bar** (acrylic/enamel red, cadmium-adjacent but with iron-oxide warmth — no bright cherry) running the full height of the composition, set off-centre, closer to one edge than the other. Add **hand-scratched or hand-stencilled numerals** — roman numerals or a countdown sequence — running vertically adjacent to or overlapping the red bar; render them imperfectly, as if stencilled with a worn template. Include **one short line of te reo Māori** (or te reo / Tagalog / English stacked, as appropriate to the course kaupapa) in industrial-stencil block capitals, reversed or mirrored if you want to invoke the protest-era Aramoana grammar, or upright for directness. Apply a **corrugated iron or weathered metal texture** to the ground using a photographic overlay at low opacity — not decorative, but referencing the actual substrate of protest. Keep any additional text to a minimum: one line, maximum, rendered in loose cursive or stencil (not polished sans-serif). **Avoid:** gloss digital perfection; bright multi-colour palettes; clean geometric sans-serif (especially Helvetica-style neutrality); anything that reads as cheerful, decorative, or sentimental; high-definition photography overlaid on the black (flatten, don't illustrate); symmetrical compositions; centred text. The work must feel like it has been *made under duress*, with a will to resist — spare, dark, purposeful, scarred by the political weight it carries. The red should feel like fire seen through a window at night, not like a warning sign. The text should feel like it was scratched into the surface, not printed onto it.
