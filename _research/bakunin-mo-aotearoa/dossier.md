## LESSON 1: Ko wai a Bakunin? — The Man Marx Feared

### 1.1 Biographical Sketch (1814–1876)

Mikhail Alexandrovich Bakunin was born on 30 May 1814 (Old Style: 18 May) on his family's estate of Premukhino in Tver province, Russia, and died on 1 July 1876 in Berne, Switzerland ([Wikipedia: Mikhail Bakunin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Bakunin)). He came from minor Russian nobility and began his public intellectual life through the Berlin Hegelian circles of the late 1830s, before moving toward active revolutionary politics.

The key biographical stations for course purposes:

- **1842**: Publishes "The Reaction in Germany" under the pseudonym Jules Elysard in Arnold Ruge's *Deutsche Jahrbücher für Wissenschaft und Kunst* (Leipzig, October 17–21, 1842). This is Bakunin's emergence from pure philosophy into revolutionary politics — the essay contains the famous "creative destruction" sentence ([Marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bakunin/works/1842/reaction-germany.htm)).
- **1848–1849**: Active in the Prague uprising (June 1848) and the Dresden insurrection (May 1849), fighting alongside Richard Wagner. Arrested, imprisoned in Austria, then extradited to Russia.
- **1851–1857**: Imprisoned in the Peter and Paul Fortress and Schlüsselburg. Petitions Tsar Nicholas I with a confessional document (*Confession*) that later caused controversy on the left.
- **1857–1861**: Internal exile in Siberia, from which he escapes via Japan and the United States in 1861, reaching London. Resumes contact with the émigré socialist world.
- **1864**: The First International (International Workingmen's Association, IWA) is founded in London (28 September 1864); Marx is central to its founding documents ([Wikipedia: First International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_International)).
- **1868**: Bakunin joins the IWA and simultaneously founds the International Alliance of Socialist Democracy, which immediately creates conflict with the General Council.
- **1869**: Bakunin translates the Communist Manifesto into Russian (the first Russian translation), demonstrating ideological proximity to, not simple opposition to, Marx — the conflict is about *organisation and the state*, not about capitalism versus reform.
- **1871**: Paris Commune (March–May 1871). Both Marx (*The Civil War in France*) and Bakunin defend it, but draw opposite lessons. Bakunin writes "The Paris Commune and the Idea of the State."
- **1872**: The Hague Congress (2–7 September 1872). Bakunin and James Guillaume are expelled from the IWA on charges of membership in a secret society (the Alliance). The vote is 49 in favour of expulsion, 14 against, 10 abstentions ([Wikipedia: Hague Congress (1872)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hague_Congress_(1872))). The IWA splits: the Bakuninist Anti-Authoritarian International forms at the Saint-Imier conference the same year.
- **1873**: *Statism and Anarchy* (*Gosudarstvennost' i Anarkhiya*) — Bakunin's last major completed work, written in Russian. This is his mature theoretical statement: critique of the state as such, critique of pan-Germanism, critique of Marx's concept of the "workers' state," and positive programme for a federated society of free communes ([Marxists.org: Statism and Anarchy](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bakunin/works/1873/statism-anarchy.htm)).
- **1876**: Dies in Berne, 1 July 1876, in poverty and relative isolation. *God and the State*, compiled from manuscripts, is published posthumously in 1882 by Élisée Reclus and Carlo Cafiero.

### 1.2 Key Works and Their Arguments

**"The Reaction in Germany" (1842)**

The earliest political text. Signed "Jules Elysard" (a Frenchman — the pseudonym underlines Bakunin's sense that German radicalism needed a foreign interlocutor to say what it could not say about itself). Written under Hegelian influence: the essay argues the political world is divided between those who affirm the *status quo* ("positivists") and those who negate it ("negativists"), and that the negativists — the radicals — are historically in the right because history's momentum is on the side of freedom. The famous closing sentence, discussed in Lesson 3, appears here ([Marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bakunin/works/1842/reaction-germany.htm)).

**"The Paris Commune and the Idea of the State" (1871)**

Written as a preamble to *The Knouto-Germanic Empire and the Social Revolution*. Bakunin takes the Commune as a model of spontaneous, non-state working-class self-governance — not a model *for* taking state power, but a demonstration that workers can organise themselves *without* the state. He criticises the Jacobin wing of the Commune for clinging to state forms, and praises the federalist, anti-authoritarian wing ([Marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bakunin/works/1871/paris-commune.htm)).

Key passage: "The Paris Commune lasted only two months, but the principles it created are indestructible... it was a bold, clearly formulated negation of the State." (trans. Dolgoff, *Bakunin on Anarchy*, 1971)

**Statism and Anarchy (1873)**

The mature synthesis. Marx read this closely enough to write a detailed *Conspectus* (1874) — a running commentary on Bakunin's arguments — which itself shows how seriously he took the challenge. Core arguments:

1. Any state — even a "workers' state" — is a structure of domination. A state ruled by former workers is still a state, and will reproduce domination.
2. German Social Democracy (and by implication, Marxism) will tend toward bureaucratic statism because of its organisational logic.
3. The correct form of socialist organisation is the federation of free communes and associations, from below upward, never the state devolving power downward.
4. Pan-Slavic federalism could be a radical force if directed against all empires, including Russia.

Direct quote from *Statism and Anarchy* (trans. Shatz, Cambridge UP 1990, p. 178): "Freedom can be created only by freedom, that is, by a universal revolt of the people and free organization of the toiling masses from the bottom up." ([Anarchist Library](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/mikhail-bakunin-statism-and-anarchy))

**God and the State (1882, posthumous)**

Never completed as a single text; compiled from manuscripts by Reclus and Cafiero. The central argument: God and the State are structurally identical — both demand submission, both suppress human reason, both are sustained by institutions that legitimate inequality. The only consistent revolutionary is the atheist who refuses both divine and earthly authority. This is Bakunin's most widely read work in English translation ([Psicopolis/God and State full text](https://www.psicopolis.com/filosofia/boxpdf/bak1a.pdf)).

Key passage (Chapter I): "Yes, our first ancestors, our Adams and our Eves, were, if not gorillas, very near relatives of gorillas, omnivorous, intelligent and ferocious beasts, endowed in a higher degree than the animals of another species with two precious faculties — the power to think and the desire to rebel." This "desire to rebel" becomes the foundation of his politics.

### 1.3 The First International Split and Why Marx Feared Bakunin

The Hague Congress of 1872 was not primarily a theoretical debate — it was a contest for organisational control of the international workers' movement. As the *Socialist Democracy Online* analysis notes, the Bakuninist federations were growing faster than the Marxist General Council's sphere of influence: "the Spanish [federation was] the largest of all [the IWA sections], Italian, Belgian, and Jurassian federations" supported Bakunin's tendency. Marx and his allies, controlling the General Council in London, chose the location of the Hague deliberately — far from the Bakuninist strongholds in Southern Europe — and arrived with bloc-voted proxies ([SDOnline](https://sdonline.org/issue/65/common-banner-marxists-and-anarchists-first-international)).

The charges against Bakunin (secret society membership) were a pretext. The real issues were:

1. **Organisational form**: Bakunin's Alliance operated as a network of loosely affiliated local groups with no central command. Marx's General Council sought disciplined, centralised coordination.
2. **The question of the state**: Could socialists use state power as a transitional instrument toward liberation? Marx said yes. Bakunin said no — any seizure of state power would corrupt the movement.
3. **Timing and tactics**: Bakunin believed the peasantry and lumpenproletariat had revolutionary potential. Marx focused on the industrial proletariat.
4. **Personality**: Both men were forceful, both accumulated loyalties. Their correspondence, as the Wikipedia article on Hague Congress documents, included mutual accusations of manipulation, dishonesty, and even, absurdly, Tsarist espionage ([Wikipedia: Hague Congress (1872)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hague_Congress_(1872))).

What "feared" means here: Marx's own *Conspectus of Bakunin's Statism and Anarchy* (1874) shows him engaging point-by-point with Bakunin's critique of the "workers' state." The fact that he wrote this private document at all — scribbling rebuttals in the margins of Bakunin's text — suggests genuine intellectual anxiety. Lenin would later have the same relationship to Bakunin's critique, producing *State and Revolution* (1917) in part as a pre-emptive answer.

### 1.4 Anarchist-Collectivism Distinguished

Bakunin's position is properly called *anarchist-collectivism* (or collectivist anarchism) — distinguished from:
- **Anarcho-communism** (Kropotkin): collective ownership *and* distribution according to need, without exchange. Bakunin retained wage labour based on contribution within the collective.
- **Anarcho-individualism** (Stirner, Tucker): primacy of individual sovereignty, minimal mutual obligation.
- **Mutualism** (Proudhon): exchange-based, workers retain product of their individual labour.

Bakunin's collectivism: the means of production held collectively by the workers who use them; wages paid according to work contributed; local communes federate voluntarily into regional bodies, regional bodies into national/international bodies — always from below, never by delegation of power downward from above. The state is not reformed; it is dissolved.

Bakunin's own statement from *Bakunin's Writings*, excerpted in Maximoff ed. (1953): "I mean that liberty of each which is not limited or restrained or curtailed by the liberty of another, but is strengthened and enlarged through it: the unlimited liberty of each through the liberty of all, liberty through solidarity, liberty in equality." ([Marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bakunin/works/writings/ch11.htm))

## LESSON 2: Mana Motuhake — Federation from Below

*Guardrail note: This lesson draws resonances between Bakunin's federalist principle and Māori political philosophy as expressed in He Whakaputanga, Te Tiriti, and Waitangi Tribunal jurisprudence. These are not equivalences. Bakunin's federalism is rooted in European revolutionary socialist theory and universalist class politics. Māori rangatiratanga is rooted in whakapapa, tikanga, and specific historical relationships between peoples and land. The resonances are real and worth thinking through; the differences are equally real and must not be collapsed. All claims about Māori philosophy in this lesson are cited from Māori scholars directly.*

### 2.1 Bakunin's Federalist Principle

The most complete statement of Bakunin's federalism is in *Federalism, Socialism, Anti-Theologism* (1867), presented to the League for Peace and Freedom congress in Geneva. The text was originally lost; Bakunin reconstructed it as an unfinished speech-text ([Anarchist Library: Federalism, Socialism, Anti-Theologism](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/michail-bakunin-federalism-socialism-anti-theologism)).

Core principle: legitimate political organisation can only be built from the bottom up. The smallest unit — the commune, the local association, the village — is politically primary. It voluntarily associates with neighbouring communes to form a regional federation. Regional federations voluntarily associate to form larger bodies. At no level does a higher body possess authority over the foundational commune that it did not receive, revocably, from below.

This is not mere decentralisation within a state — it is the abolition of the state as such. The state, for Bakunin, is defined by *downward delegation*: it claims sovereign authority and parcels out (or withholds) rights to those below it. The Bakuninist federation inverts this: authority originates in the commune and is delegated upward, conditionally, for specific coordination purposes.

From *Statism and Anarchy* (1873), trans. Shatz: "The federative principle... is what Bakunin calls the negation of the state... instead of a state above society, a free federation of local self-governing bodies." (paraphrase; for extended passage see [Marxists.org: Statism and Anarchy](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bakunin/works/1873/statism-anarchy.htm))

From *Federalism, Socialism, Anti-Theologism* (1867): "It must replace the principle of authority with the principle of freedom... replace government and legislation by the free federation of agricultural and industrial associations." ([Anarchist Library](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/michail-bakunin-federalism-socialism-anti-theologism))

From *Marxism, Freedom and the State* (1870–1872), ed. Kenafick: "The organisation of the society from below upwards, by means of free association or federation of the workers." ([Anarchist Library](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/mikhail-bakunin-marxism-freedom-and-the-state))

### 2.2 He Whakaputanga o te Rangatiratanga o Nu Tireni (1835)

He Whakaputanga — the Declaration of Independence of the United Tribes of New Zealand — was signed by 34 rangatira at Waitangi on 28 October 1835, following a hui convened by the British resident James Busby ([Archives New Zealand](https://www.archives.govt.nz/discover-our-stories/the-declaration-of-independence-of-new-zealand)). The number of signatories grew to over 50 rangatira by 1839 ([NZ History: Declaration of Independence](https://nzhistory.govt.nz/culture/declaration-of-independence-taming-the-frontier)).

He Whakaputanga asserts:
- The sovereign power and authority of the United Tribes is unqualified (Article 1)
- Legislative power is vested exclusively in the assembled rangatira of the Confederation (Article 2)
- The Confederation will meet annually to frame laws (Article 3)
- The King of England is invited as a protector and friend — not as a sovereign (Article 4)

The document is significant for this course because it represents rangatira articulating, on their own terms and prior to British colonisation, a federated governance structure: multiple independent sovereign entities voluntarily associating for common purposes while retaining their own authority ([Archives NZ](https://www.archives.govt.nz/discover-our-stories/the-declaration-of-independence-of-new-zealand)).

**Resonance with Bakunin's federalism**: Both He Whakaputanga and Bakunin's federalist principle conceive of legitimate political authority as residing in the constituent unit (the hapū/commune) and associating voluntarily upward. Both reject the idea that sovereignty can be delegated *down* from an external authority to constituent bodies.

**Critical difference**: Bakunin's federalism is a universal class-based theory addressed to European industrial workers. He Whakaputanga is not a theory — it is a specific historical assertion of rangatiratanga by specific peoples in relation to specific lands, grounded in whakapapa and tikanga. The rangatira were not theorising away from the state; they were asserting their pre-existing authority against colonial encroachment. The political logic is structurally similar in one dimension; the foundations, history, and stakes are entirely different.

### 2.3 Te Tiriti o Waitangi (1840) and the Kawanatanga / Rangatiratanga Distinction

Te Tiriti o Waitangi was signed beginning 6 February 1840. The key jurisprudential point for this course: the te reo Māori text and the English text differ materially, and the difference is not merely linguistic but constitutive of the ongoing Treaty dispute ([Te Ara](https://teara.govt.nz/en/te-tiriti-o-waitangi-the-treaty-of-waitangi/print)).

- The English text purports to cede "sovereignty" to the Crown.
- The te reo text uses *kawanatanga* (governorship/governance) for the Crown's role, and *tino rangatiratanga* (full chieftainship; absolute authority) for the rights retained by Māori.

Since over 500 rangatira ultimately signed (most signing the te reo text), the Māori understanding — that they were granting governorship for certain purposes while retaining their own authority — is the legally and historically primary understanding, as consistently argued by Māori legal scholars.

Moana Jackson, in his analysis of the UN Special Rapporteur's 2006 report on the foreshore and seabed issue, articulated this clearly: the Crown has consistently attempted to convert *kawanatanga* (which it received) into full sovereignty (which it did not receive). The foreshore and seabed legislation of 2004 was one such attempt ([Moana Jackson, converge.org.nz](https://www.converge.org.nz/pma/mj050406.htm)).

Jackson's 2022 essay "The Myth of Sovereignty" (University of Auckland) extends this: the very concept of "sovereignty" as used in Western political theory is a myth — it assumes a centre of absolute authority that can be fully held and transferred. Māori rangatiratanga is not a version of that concept but a different political ontology, grounded in relationship, reciprocity, and whakapapa ([University of Auckland](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2022/11/02/the-myth-of-sovereignty.html)).

**Note for Robert**: This is a crucial point of difference. Bakunin also critiques sovereignty — the state's claim to absolute authority — but from within the European tradition. Jackson's critique goes deeper: he questions whether the Western concept of sovereignty is coherent or desirable at all. This is not the same argument. Use both, mark the difference.

### 2.4 Wai 262 / Ko Aotearoa Tēnei (2011)

The Wai 262 claim, filed in 1991 by six claimants representing Te Rarawa, Ngāti Kuri, Ngāti Wai, Te Whanau-ā-Apanui, Ngāti Kahungunu, and Ngāti Koata, is the broadest claim ever heard by the Waitangi Tribunal. It concerns Crown law and policy affecting Māori culture and identity: intellectual property, the Māori language, flora and fauna, traditional knowledge, and the environment ([Wai 262 site](https://www.wai262.nz/ko-aotearoa-tenei)).

*Ko Aotearoa Tēnei* (released 2 July 2011) is described by the Tribunal as its "first report... covering all of government" — spanning over 20 government departments and agencies. Key findings:

- The Treaty obliges the Crown to actively protect taonga (treasures) Māori value, including the Māori language and living cultural practices.
- Crown law and policy has systematically failed to honour these obligations.
- The report calls for "a new era based on partnership" rather than continued grievance resolution ([waitangitribunal.govt.nz](https://waitangitribunal.govt.nz/mi/news/ko-aotearoa-tenei-report-on-the-wai-262-claim-released)).

For this course, the key section is the report's treatment of *mana motuhake* — autonomous Māori governance over Māori cultural assets. This is not a demand to create new institutions from nothing; it is a demand that the Crown stop actively undermining Māori governance structures that pre-exist colonisation.

**Bakunin resonance**: Bakunin's federalism insists that legitimate authority rests in communities that pre-exist the state and are not creatures of it. The Waitangi Tribunal, in *Ko Aotearoa Tēnei*, makes a structurally similar claim in specific legal and historical terms: Māori governance structures (hapū, iwi, marae) pre-exist the Crown and retain authority that was not surrendered by Te Tiriti. Again: same structural logic, entirely different foundations.

### 2.5 Scholars to Cite Directly

**Moana Jackson (Ngāti Kahungunu, Ngāti Porou, 1945–2022)**: The foremost Māori constitutional lawyer of his generation. His work consistently argued for constitutional transformation — not reform of the existing constitutional settlement but its replacement with one that genuinely honours rangatiratanga. His phrase "constitutional transformation" is careful: transformation, not just amendment. Do not paraphrase Jackson; quote him or cite him. Sources: [converge.org.nz (2006)](https://www.converge.org.nz/pma/mj050406.htm); [University of Auckland (2022)](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2022/11/02/the-myth-of-sovereignty.html); [Natlib interview](https://natlib.govt.nz/he-tohu/korero/interview-with-moana-jackson).

**Margaret Mutu (Ngāti Kahu)**: Professor of Māori Studies at University of Auckland; co-claimant in Wai 262; a leading scholar of Treaty law and Māori rights. Her interview with E-Tangata on co-governance: "call it what you want, co-governance isn't going away" ([E-Tangata](https://e-tangata.co.nz/comment-and-analysis/margaret-mutu-call-it-what-you-want-co-governance-isnt-going-away/)). Her brief of evidence to the Waitangi Tribunal is in the public record ([Waitangi Tribunal documents](https://forms.justice.govt.nz/search/Documents/WT/wt_DOC_213861680/Wai%203300,%203.3.011.pdf)).

**Ani Mikaere (Ngāti Raukawa, Waikato-Tainui)**: Scholar of Māori jurisprudence and tikanga. Her work challenges the assimilation of tikanga Māori into common law frameworks — an argument that has structural resonance with Bakunin's refusal to assimilate workers' self-organisation into state legal frameworks.

## LESSON 3: Te Tāheke — The Passion for Destruction Is Also a Creative Passion

### 3.1 The Source: "The Reaction in Germany" (1842)

The famous sentence appears in the concluding section of "Die Reaktion in Deutschland. Ein Fragment von einem Franzosen," published in *Deutsche Jahrbücher für Wissenschaft und Kunst*, nos. 247–51, Leipzig, October 17–21, 1842, signed "Jules Elysard." ([Marxists.org full text](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bakunin/works/1842/reaction-germany.htm))

The text as given on Marxists.org (source note: from [Anarchist Library](https://theanarchistlibrary.org)):

> "Let us therefore trust the eternal Spirit which destroys and annihilates only because it is the unfathomable and eternally creative source of all life. The passion for destruction is a creative passion, too!"

The Wikiquote entry for Bakunin renders this slightly differently (various translations):

> "The urge to destroy is also a creative urge!" (alternate translation)

([en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Mikhail_Bakunin](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Mikhail_Bakunin))

**Bibliographic precision**: The essay was written October 1842 and published in the *Deutsche Jahrbücher* under Bakunin's pseudonym "Jules Elysard." The pseudonym was a French form — Bakunin was asserting a French republican perspective against German conservative Hegelianism. The essay is described on the Marxists.org page as marking "Bakunin's emergence from purely philosophical studies to active participation in revolutionary sociopolitical movements."

The editorial note on Marxists.org further specifies: "Its polemics are aimed at the 'compromisers' — who Bakunin called 'positivists' as opposed to the radical 'negativists' with regard, of course, to the *status quo* or establishment — and the radicals." The essay employs Hegelian language: the dialectical logic of negation, the idea that what must be overcome contains within it the seed of what supersedes it.

### 3.2 What "Destruction" Meant for Bakunin

This is not nihilism. Nihilism (as theorised by Turgenev, practised by Nechayev, and influencing some Russian revolutionary currents of the 1860s–1870s) held that existing social values were false and must be utterly negated, without necessarily specifying what comes next. Bakunin explicitly and repeatedly separated himself from pure nihilism — indeed, he critiqued Nechayev's *Revolutionary Catechism* (co-authored, though Bakunin later repudiated it) as a distortion of revolutionary ethics.

For Bakunin, "destruction" means:
- The abolition of **institutions** — the state, the church, inherited property — not the killing of people
- The deliberate, reasoned dismantling of structures that reproduce domination
- The release of human creative energy that is currently captured and suppressed by those structures

The full sentence in context makes this clear. Bakunin writes of "the eternal Spirit which destroys and annihilates *only because* it is the unfathomable and eternally creative source of all life." Destruction is in service of life — the negation is dialectical, not absolute. What is negated is re-incorporated at a higher level (in Hegelian terms, *Aufhebung*: sublation — cancelling, preserving, and elevating simultaneously).

From *God and the State* (posthumous ms.): "the power to think and the desire to rebel" are the two defining faculties of the human animal — and both are suppressed by the state and the church. Liberation begins with their assertion ([Psicopolis/God and State](https://www.psicopolis.com/filosofia/boxpdf/bak1a.pdf)).

From the 1842 essay itself, the essay opens: "FREEDOM, the realization of freedom: who can deny that this expression today stands at the head of the agenda of history?" The destruction he calls for is destruction *in the name of freedom* — not destruction as an end in itself ([Marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bakunin/works/1842/reaction-germany.htm)).

### 3.3 Distinguishing Bakunin from Nihilism

| | Bakunin | Nihilism (Nechayev school) |
|---|---|---|
| **Goal of destruction** | Abolition of oppressive institutions; creation of free society | Total negation; destruction as the revolutionary act itself |
| **Role of the individual** | Individual freedom enlarged by collective freedom | Individual subordinated entirely to the revolution |
| **Ethics** | Humanism — human dignity and freedom are the end | No ethical constraints on revolutionary action |
| **The revolutionary** | Embedded in the working class, accountable to it | Secretive, vanguardist, autonomous from class |
| **After destruction** | Federated free communes; society from below | Left deliberately vague or absent |

Bakunin's own words on this distinction, from *Marxism, Freedom and the State* (ed. Kenafick): he argues that genuine revolution must be made *by* the people, not *for* them, and that any organisation which places itself above the people to lead them in their liberation will merely replicate the hierarchy it claims to destroy ([Anarchist Library](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/mikhail-bakunin-marxism-freedom-and-the-state)).

### 3.4 The Tāheke Motif: What Falls Feeds the Awa

**Pedagogical framing** (for Robert's voice — do not treat this as Māori scholarship, treat it as a working-class Pākehā poet's metaphor drawing on Aotearoa landscape):

The tāheke — the waterfall — is a productive destructive force. Water that was still, captured in a form, releases that form in its fall. It does not cease to exist; it enters the awa (river) in a new condition, with new energy, able to move further. The sound (*tangi*) of the tāheke is not just destruction — it is announcement; it is presence; it is the voice of becoming.

This is not a Māori cultural claim — it is a Pākehā writer using a feature of Aotearoa geography as a structural metaphor. If you want to connect this to Māori thinking, you would need to cite a Māori source on how tāheke functions in te ao Māori — and be explicit that you are seeking a connection, not asserting one.

What the metaphor does:
- Localises Bakunin's European-abstract concept in Aotearoa landscape
- Makes destruction productive (the water does not disappear; it feeds what comes after)
- Frames the course title: *Tangi o te Tāheke* — the sound/lament of the waterfall, the voice of the necessary destruction that precedes new life

The poutama pattern (staircase ascending) can serve as counterpoint: the tāheke falls while the poutama rises. Both involve transformation. The course's six thinkers are a poutama — each one lifts the kaupapa a step higher. Bakunin is the tāheke: he insists the stairs themselves must sometimes be broken to be rebuilt rightly.

## LESSON 4: Caracoles in the Kāinga — A Zapatista Reading

### 4.1 EZLN History: January 1, 1994

The Zapatista Army of National Liberation (EZLN — *Ejército Zapatista de Liberación Nacional*) launched its armed uprising in Chiapas, Mexico on January 1, 1994 — precisely the day the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA) came into force ([Wikipedia: Zapatista Uprising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zapatista_uprising)).

The timing was deliberate: NAFTA's Article 27 had revised the Mexican constitution to allow the privatisation of communal ejido (collectively farmed) lands — directly threatening the land base on which indigenous communities in Chiapas depended. The EZLN declared war on the Mexican government, seized several towns in Chiapas (including San Cristóbal de las Casas), and issued its First Declaration of the Lacandon Jungle.

Key facts for the course:
- The EZLN is composed primarily of indigenous Maya peoples (Tzeltal, Tzotzil, Tojolabal, Chol) from Chiapas
- The military uprising lasted approximately 12 days before a ceasefire (January 12, 1994)
- After the ceasefire, the EZLN shifted primarily to political and civil resistance
- The uprising did not aim at state capture — the Zapatistas sought autonomy, recognition of indigenous rights, and democratic accountability, not to govern Mexico ([Britannica: EZLN](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Zapatista-National-Liberation-Army))

The Zapatista slogan "Ya Basta!" ("Enough already!") resonated internationally as a declaration against neoliberalism, globalisation, and indigenous dispossession.

### 4.2 Caracoles and the Juntas de Buen Gobierno (2003)

The 2003 reorganisation was a major moment in Zapatista autonomy-building. In August 2003, the EZLN dissolved the Aguascalientes (community cultural centres) and replaced them with five *caracoles* (spiral shells) as autonomous administrative centres. Simultaneously, they established the *Juntas de Buen Gobierno* (Good Government Councils, JBG) at each caracol ([SIPAZ archive](https://www.sipaz.org/coyuntura-resistencia-y-autonomia-creacion-de-las-juntas-de-buen-gobierno-zapatistas/)).

Subcommander Marcos, in his address at Oventik on August 9, 2003 (the birth of the Juntas), described this as "a very important advance in our struggle for the recognition of the rights and culture of indigenous peoples in Mexico" ([Enlace Zapatista](https://enlacezapatista.ezln.org.mx/2003/08/09/subcomandante-marcos-palabras-por-el-nacimiento-de-las-juntas-de-buen-gobierno/)).

The JBGs were designed to:
- Coordinate between the Zapatista Rebel Autonomous Municipalities (MAREZ)
- Manage relationships with NGOs, international solidarity groups, and the Mexican civil society
- Receive and distribute resources equitably
- Adjudicate disputes between communities
- Rotate membership to prevent entrenchment of power

The caracol (snail/spiral) as motif: the spiral moves outward from the centre while always returning to it; it implies depth rather than linear expansion; it is the opposite of the state's hierarchical pyramid.

**Mandar obedeciendo** (to govern by obeying): This is the Zapatista political principle — leaders govern only insofar as they obey the mandate of the people who elected them. Authority flows from the community up to the leadership, not from the leadership down. Any JBG member who acts against the community's mandate can be immediately recalled. ([Illwill: Reorganization of Zapatista Autonomy](https://illwill.com/the-reorganization-of-zapatista-autonomy))

### 4.3 The Sixth Declaration of the Lacandon Jungle (2005)

The Sixth Declaration, issued June 28, 2005 (commonly called "La Sexta"), is the EZLN's most comprehensive political manifesto. Key passages ([Schools for Chiapas PDF](https://schoolsforchiapas.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/03/6th-Declaration-of-the-Lacandona-Jungle.pdf)):

"This is our simple word which seeks to touch the hearts of humble and simple people like ourselves, but people who are also, like ourselves, dignified and rebel... in order to invite other persons to walk with us in something very great which is called Mexico and something greater which is called the world."

The document announces the *Otra Campaña* (Other Campaign) — a national listening tour where the EZLN would stop talking and start listening to communities across Mexico. The Zapatistas would not lead, run for election, or take state power. They would build connections between struggles.

The Sixth Declaration is explicitly anti-vanguardist: it refuses the claim that the EZLN has the answers and others must follow. It invites "those who are like us" — poor, humble, rebellious — to "join together with them, everywhere they are living and struggling."

Structurally, the Sixth Declaration is Bakuninist without naming Bakunin: it refuses the state, insists on autonomous organisation, builds from the local upward, and explicitly declines to capture power ([Wikipedia: Sixth Declaration of the Lacandon Jungle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sixth_Declaration_of_the_Lacandon_Jungle)).

### 4.4 The 2023 Reorganisation: GAL/CGAL/ACGAZ

In November 2023, Subcommander Moisés released a series of communiqués outlining a fundamental reorganisation of Zapatista autonomy, prompted by internal evaluation of what had worked and what had not in the MAREZ/JBG system ([Enlace Zapatista: Ninth Part](https://enlacezapatista.ezln.org.mx/2023/11/13/ninth-part-the-new-structure-of-zapastista-autonomy/)).

The new structure as described by Moisés:

1. **GAL (Gobiernos Autónomos Locales / Local Autonomous Governments)**: The foundational unit. "There is a GAL in each community where Zapatista support bases live. The Zapatista GALs are the core of all autonomy. They are coordinated by autonomous agents and commissioners and are subject to the assembly of the town, ranchería, community, area, neighborhood, ejido, colony, or however each population calls itself." (Direct translation, [Enlace Zapatista](https://enlacezapatista.ezln.org.mx/2023/11/13/ninth-part-the-new-structure-of-zapastista-autonomy/))

   "So, if before there were a few dozen MAREZ, that is, Zapatista Rebel Autonomous Municipalities, now there are thousands of Zapatista GALs."

2. **CGAZ (Colectivos de Gobierno Autónomo Zapatista / Collectives of Zapatista Autonomous Government)**: Regional coordination of GALs. "The CGAZ are formed in each community or zone where several Zapatista communities agree to coordinate."

3. **ACGAZ (Asambleas de Colectivos de Gobierno Autónomo Zapatista)**: Zone-level assemblies. Headquartered in the Caracoles. They can convene zone-level assemblies when GAL and CGAZ deem it necessary.

Jérôme Baschet (in Illwill) interprets this as a radical deepening of the bottom-up principle: "the nucleus of all autonomy" is now the *community-level* assembly, not the municipality. The scale has inverted — from dozens of municipalities to thousands of communities. ([Illwill](https://illwill.com/the-reorganization-of-zapatista-autonomy))

The motivation, per Moisés: address security threats from paramilitary groups, narco-violence, and state encroachment; allow each community to control its own resources and relationships; reduce the bureaucratisation that had developed in the JBG system; respond to attacks on individual communities more quickly without waiting for zone-level coordination.

### 4.5 Application to Aotearoa: The Kāinga Model

**Sites of comparison** (parallels, not equivalences — the historical, legal, and cultural contexts are entirely different):

| Zapatista structure | Possible Aotearoa analogue | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| GAL (community assembly) | Marae community meeting / marae trustees | Marae is already a federated governance structure with its own tikanga; not a creation of the state |
| CGAZ (regional collective) | Rūnanga / tribal authority coordination | Iwi and hapū structures already function as networks; question is *on whose terms* they coordinate |
| Caracol (zone centre) | Community hub / kāinga hou | Could be a papakāinga project, community land trust, or workers' co-operative centre |
| JBG principles (mandar obedeciendo) | Pātaka economy / learning circle accountability | The principle — leaders accountable and recallable by the community — has resonance in kaupapa Māori pedagogy and in cooperative governance |
| Pátaka zapatista (mutual-aid storehouses) | Pātaka (food store) / community food network | Literal analogue: the Zapatista *pátaka* model of community resource storage maps to the pātaka concept in Aotearoa |

**Critical limits of the comparison**:

1. The EZLN arose in a context of armed insurgency against a Mexican state that was actively repressing indigenous communities. Aotearoa's context is substantially different: Māori rights claims are being pursued through Treaty jurisprudence, Waitangi Tribunal processes, and constitutional debate, as well as direct action. The level of state violence is different.

2. The Zapatista communities are in communities that have been autonomous for 30 years; they have built institutions from the ground up over that time. Pākehā working-class communities in Aotearoa do not have that history; Māori communities do — but that is their history and their institutions, not a template that can be borrowed.

3. The Zapatista model emerged from a specific indigenous Maya context with its own political philosophy, cosmology, and social organisation. It cannot simply be transplanted; it can be learned from.

**What the Zapatista reading offers for AI Warrior**:

- A concrete, working example of *federation from below* that has lasted 30+ years
- Evidence that autonomous governance structures can provide health, education, and justice without state support
- The principle of mandar obedeciendo as a practical accountability mechanism applicable to any study circle, co-op, or community organisation
- The caracol/spiral as a visual and conceptual tool: movements that return to deepen rather than movements that expand to conquer

## LESSON 5: Refusing the Algorithmic Leviathan — Anarchism in the Age of AI

### 5.1 Bakunin's Critique of the State as Machine

*God and the State* (1882), chapters I–IV, offers the most concentrated version of Bakunin's anti-statism. The argument: the state, like the church, is a mechanism for concentrating power and legitimating domination through impersonal authority. Both claim to speak in the name of something higher (God; the Nation; the Law) to exempt themselves from the accountability they impose on others ([God and State full text](https://www.psicopolis.com/filosofia/boxpdf/bak1a.pdf)).

Key passage from *God and the State*: "All religions, with their gods, their demigods, and their prophets, their messiahs and their saints, were created by the credulous fancy of men who had not attained the full development and full possession of their faculties. Consequently, the religious heaven is nothing but a mirage in which man, exalted by ignorance and faith, discovers his own image, but enlarged and reversed — that is, divinized."

Bakunin extends this: the State performs the same ideological function as God — it presents a human power arrangement as if it were a natural or transcendent fact, and punishes those who refuse submission. The bureaucracy is the priesthood of the secular state.

From *Bakunin's Writings* (Maximoff ed., Marxists.org): "I love freedom with all my heart. It is the only condition under which the intelligence, the manliness, and happiness of the people, can develop and expand. By freedom, however, I naturally understand not its more [formal] form, forced down as from above, measured and controlled by the state, this eternal lie which, in reality, is nothing but the privilege of the few founded upon the slavery of all." ([Marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bakunin/works/writings/ch11.htm))

The structural critique is simple: any institution that claims the right to manage and surveil others without being managed and surveilled in return is reproducing the logic of domination, regardless of its ostensible political content. The workers' state, the welfare state, the AI-enabled state — all apply the same logic.

### 5.2 James C. Scott: Seeing Like a State

James C. Scott's *Seeing Like a State: How Certain Schemes to Improve the Human Condition Have Failed* (Yale UP, 1998) provides the most useful social-science elaboration of Bakunin's structural critique for contemporary application ([Wikipedia: Seeing Like a State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seeing_Like_a_State)).

Scott's central concept: **legibility**. The modern state requires that the social world be rendered legible — simplified, standardised, and measurable — so that it can be managed from the centre. This means:
- Standardising surnames, addresses, and units of measurement
- Converting mixed-use commons into monocultures
- Replacing diverse local knowledge with uniform technocratic protocols
- Building cities on grids rather than organic patterns

The drive for legibility destroys the complex, adaptive *mētis* (local practical knowledge) that makes communities resilient. High-modernist schemes (Soviet collectivisation, scientific forestry, urban planning) that ignore local knowledge in favour of top-down rationality consistently fail — and kill.

The connection to AI is direct: algorithmic management systems are the apotheosis of legibility — they reduce human social life to data points that can be aggregated, sorted, and optimised from a remote centre. The individual is legible to the algorithm but the algorithm is not legible to the individual. This is Bakunin's state-machine critique made computational.

Scott's work is also directly applicable to the question of Māori data sovereignty: the colonial project in Aotearoa was precisely a project of legibility — rendering Māori land, people, and social structures legible to the Crown so they could be managed (and dispossessed). The continuing demand for Māori data to flow into Crown-controlled systems is a continuation of that project.

### 5.3 Shoshana Zuboff: Surveillance Capitalism

Shoshana Zuboff's *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism: The Fight for a Human Future at the New Machine Frontier* (PublicAffairs, 2019) names the economic logic of the algorithmic Leviathan: the extraction of behavioural data from human activity as raw material for the prediction and modification of behaviour ([Wikipedia: The Age of Surveillance Capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Age_of_Surveillance_Capitalism)).

Key concepts for this lesson:
- **Behavioural surplus**: Data about human behaviour beyond what is needed to improve the product — captured, analysed, and sold to predict (and shape) future behaviour
- **Instrumentation power**: The capacity to nudge, cue, and modify behaviour at scale through algorithmically optimised interfaces
- **The unilateral claim**: Platform corporations claim the right to surveil without consent, without reciprocity, without democratic accountability

Zuboff's testimony to the US House of Representatives (2022) describes this as "a new form of power" that has "migrated outside of democratic reach" — surveillance capitalism "operates through unprecedented asymmetries of knowledge, and the power that accrues from knowledge" ([Zuboff House testimony](https://docs.house.gov/meetings/HA/HA00/20220216/114403/HHRG-117-HA00-Wstate-ZuboffS-20220216.pdf)).

The Bakunin connection: Zuboff describes the platform corporation exercising precisely the power Bakunin attributes to the state — unilateral, asymmetric, claiming legitimacy from above, resistant to accountability from below. The platform *is* a state-form, operating outside formal state structures but exercising equivalent (or greater) power over daily life.

### 5.4 Te Mana Raraunga and Māori Data Sovereignty

Te Mana Raraunga (Māori Data Sovereignty Network) was established in 2015–2016. Its *Charter* (2016) is the founding document ([Te Mana Raraunga Charter PDF](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/58e9b10f9de4bb8d1fb5ebbc/t/5913020d15cf7dde1df34482/1494417935052/Te+Mana+Raraunga+Charter+(Final+&+Approved).pdf)).

Direct quotes from the Charter:
- "Data is a living tāonga and is of strategic value to Māori."
- "Māori data refers to data produced by Māori or that is about Māori and the environments we have relationships with."
- "Māori Data Sovereignty recognises that Māori data should be subject to Māori governance."
- "Māori Data Sovereignty supports tribal sovereignty and the realisation of Maori and Iwi aspirations."
- "Māori data is subject to the rights articulated in the Treaty of Waitangi and the UN's Declaration on the rights of Indigenous Peoples, to which Aotearoa New Zealand is a signatory."

The Charter cites Wai 262, the Mataatua Declaration, and UNDRIP as its legal foundations — grounding Māori data sovereignty in established indigenous rights frameworks.

The Royal Society of New Zealand / Te Mana Raraunga report *Mana Raraunga: Data Sovereignty* (2019) elaborates: "Indigenous data sovereignty is responsive to the collective," "Indigenous data sovereignty is a response to the past and a vision for the future." The report specifically discusses algorithms and artificial intelligence as areas where Māori data sovereignty principles must apply ([Royal Society NZ PDF](https://www.royalsociety.org.nz/assets/Mana-Raraunga-Data-Sovereignty-web-V1.pdf)).

**The Bakunin–data sovereignty connection**: Bakunin's federalism asserts that legitimate authority originates in communities and is delegated upward, conditionally. Te Mana Raraunga makes a structurally analogous assertion about data: it originates in communities and should be governed by those communities, not by the Crown or by corporate platforms that claim it as raw material. "Māori data should be subject to Māori governance" is, in the domain of data, a federalist principle — not because it comes from Bakunin, but because the underlying logic (authority resides in the producing community, not in the centralising institution that processes it) is the same.

**Critical difference**: Māori data sovereignty is grounded in Treaty rights and indigenous rights law — it makes specific, legally cognisable claims. Bakunin's federalism was a political philosophy that rejected law as an instrument of liberation. These are different projects. The resonance is in the structural logic; the foundations and strategies are different.

### 5.5 Karaitiana Taiuru

Dr. Karaitiana Taiuru is the Director of the Institute of AI Governance and a Māori Data Sovereignty and AI Ethics expert with over 30 years in the IT sector ([ACE Aotearoa](https://www.aceaotearoa.org.nz/news-and-resources/news/ai-ethics-and-indigenous-data-sovereignty)).

Key themes from Taiuru's work:
- Māori communities must retain control over their digital and cultural data despite limited resources
- Data reframed as *living taonga* — not a commodity but a relationship
- The constitutional treaty (Te Tiriti) as a potential model for ethical digital sovereignty
- "It's important that we have a say in what data should be digitized and what should be left in our traditional villages. For example, much of our sacred knowledge should never [be digitised]." ([The Data Wire](https://www.thedatawire.com/news/maori-data-sovereignty-ai-governance-institute-new-zealand-karaitiana-taiuru))
- Emphasis on "mythbusting" around AI — not uncritical rejection, but informed, community-governed engagement

Taiuru's framing of the *Treaty as digital sovereignty framework* has direct implications for the NZ AI Strategy.

### 5.6 New Zealand AI Strategy (July 2025)

New Zealand's AI Strategy, *Investing with Confidence* (Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment, July 2025) — the most current policy document at time of writing — focuses primarily on "accelerating private sector AI adoption and innovation" ([MBIE](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/digital-policy/new-zealands-ai-strategy-investing-with-confidence)).

This framing is significant: the strategy's primary lens is economic productivity and investment confidence, not democratic governance or indigenous rights. The PDF is available directly from the Beehive: [NZ AI Strategy PDF](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-07/New%20Zealand's%20AI%20Strategy%20-%20Investing%20with%20confidence.pdf).

From the Bakunist/Te Mana Raraunga perspective, a strategy focused on "private sector adoption" of AI systems that process Māori and working-class New Zealanders' data — without structural mechanisms for community control, consent, or data sovereignty — reproduces the legibility logic Scott describes and the surveillance capitalism logic Zuboff names. The algorithmic Leviathan is being built with public subsidies and without democratic accountability.

**Key tensions for the course**:
1. The NZ AI Strategy's emphasis on "confidence" (for investors) vs. the confidence Māori communities need that their data will not be extracted and weaponised
2. The gap between the Treaty partnership principle and a strategy that does not centre Treaty obligations
3. The absence of robust data sovereignty mechanisms in the strategy document

## LESSON 6: Arm the Class — Closing the Thinkers Series

### 6.1 The Series: Six Thinkers, One Through-Line

The Kiwi Dialectic / AI Warrior thinkers series has traced a working-class socialist pedagogy through six thinkers, each contributing a distinct analytical tool:

### 6.2 The Through-Line

What connects these six thinkers — across their profound disagreements with one another — is the question: *how is domination reproduced, and how is it undone?*

**Gramsci** answers: domination is reproduced through consent — through the cultural, intellectual, and moral leadership (hegemony) that makes exploitation appear natural. Undone through the slow, patient building of counter-hegemonic institutions — the party, the union, the school — that build working-class capacity to govern ([Marxists.org: Harman on Gramsci](https://www.marxists.org/archive/harman/1977/06/gramsci2.html)).

**Kropotkin** answers: domination suppresses the cooperative instinct that is as natural as competition. Mutual aid is the evolutionary base of human society; liberation is the recovery of that instinct from the competitive logic imposed by capitalism ([Wikipedia: Mutual Aid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutual_Aid:_A_Factor_of_Evolution)).

**Graeber** answers: domination is reproduced through debt — the original political relation, which converts free persons into obligated ones — and through bureaucratic absurdity (bullshit jobs) that consumes human creativity in meaningless activity. Undone through debt abolition, direct democracy, and the refusal of meaningless work ([Wikipedia: David Graeber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Graeber)).

**Freire** answers: domination is reproduced through education that deposits knowledge into passive students (banking education), teaching the oppressed to internalise their own oppression. Undone through dialogue — the naming of the world, *conscientização*, the development of critical consciousness ([Everett SD: Freire Banking Concept](https://www.everettsd.org/cms/lib07/WA01920133/Centricity/Domain/947/BANKING%20CONCEPT%20OF%20ED.2.pdf)).

**Deleuze** answers: the old disciplinary power (Foucault's enclosed institutions — factory, school, hospital, prison) has given way to control societies, where power operates through continuous modulation rather than confinement. The dividual (the data profile, the credit score) rather than the individual is the unit of management. Undone by rhizomatic organisation — horizontal, multiple entry points, no centre, no trunk — against the arboreal (tree-structured) hierarchies of both capitalism and traditional left parties ([Deleuze: Postscript on Societies of Control](https://deleuze.cla.purdue.edu/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/Deleuze_Gilles-Postscript-on-the-Societies-of-Control.pdf)).

**Bakunin** answers: domination is reproduced by the state itself — by any structure that claims authority from above and manages populations from the centre. Including the workers' state. Undone only by the abolition of the state and its replacement with federated communes and associations, built from below. The path is destruction before construction: the passion for destruction is creative ([Marxists.org: Reaction in Germany](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bakunin/works/1842/reaction-germany.htm)).

### 6.3 Where They Contradict Each Other

These thinkers do not form a coherent system. The course is stronger for acknowledging the tensions:

- **Bakunin vs. Gramsci**: Gramsci accepts the state as a terrain of struggle — the long march through the institutions. Bakunin refuses — the state is not a terrain to occupy but a structure to abolish. This is not a minor tactical disagreement; it is the fundamental divide of the left for 150 years.

- **Deleuze vs. Gramsci**: Gramsci's counter-hegemonic bloc requires a leading political party — arboreal organisation. Deleuze's rhizome is explicitly hostile to that model. The tension between organised and prefigurative politics runs through both.

- **Freire vs. Bakunin**: Freire's dialogical education still requires a teacher — a transformed teacher, but still an expert who facilitates. Bakunin's anti-authoritarianism pushes toward peer learning and questions whether the pedagogical relationship can ever escape domination.

- **Graeber vs. Kropotkin**: Graeber's *Debt* shows that mutual aid has historically coexisted with extreme violence — the potlatch as competitive destruction, the gift as obligation. Kropotkin's optimism about cooperation needs Graeber's complexity.

**For AI Warrior**: the series does not deliver a finished theory. It delivers six lenses. The working-class socialist in Aotearoa in 2025 needs all six — and needs to know when to use which one.

### 6.4 Synthesis for Aotearoa: The Practical Kaupapa

Drawing the threads together for an Aotearoa working-class audience:

**Organise locally**: The marae, the union branch, the study circle, the credit union, the community garden, the repair café — these are the primary units. Not because they are charming, but because they are where relationships are built and trust is tested. Bakunin's commune, Kropotkin's mutual-aid association, Freire's dialogue circle — all start local.

**Federate widely**: Local organisation becomes powerful through connection. The Zapatista GAL model shows this: thousands of local autonomous governments, coordinating at the regional level through CGAZs, without surrendering local authority. In Aotearoa: union federations, iwi networks, co-operative alliances, cross-community mutual-aid networks. The Treaty principle of partnership — as articulated by Moana Jackson and the Waitangi Tribunal — already implies a federal relationship; the question is who controls the terms.

**Refuse the platform**: Deleuze's control society operates through the platform. Zuboff's surveillance capitalism extracts value from every click. Taiuru's data sovereignty work provides a principled framework for refusal: communities should govern their own data, not surrender it to platforms whose business model is behavioural prediction. For AI Warrior: build on federated, open-source tools where possible; audit tools you do use; develop data literacy as part of political literacy.

**Build the pātaka**: The pātaka is not just a metaphor. The practical programme is mutual aid infrastructure: food networks, time banks, tool libraries, skills exchanges, housing co-operatives, study circles. Kropotkin's mutual aid, Graeber's gift economy, the Zapatista *pátaka* — all converge on the same practical project. In Aotearoa, the pātaka already exists as concept and practice; the task is to extend and connect it.

**The Bakunin contribution to this synthesis**: He provides the refusal of state-capture as the goal. The other thinkers risk being domesticated by the state — Gramscian hegemony-building becomes parliamentary politics; Freirean pedagogy becomes school curriculum reform; Kropotkin's mutual aid becomes social service delivery. Bakunin insists: any organisation that accepts the state's terms for its own existence will eventually reproduce state logic. Build outside, build against, build *from below*.

**The limits of Bakunin in Aotearoa**: He was wrong about spontaneity — revolution does not spontaneously erupt from class oppression without organisation. Gramsci was right to insist on the slow, patient work of building counter-hegemonic capacity. Freire was right to insist on dialogue and critical consciousness as prerequisites for transformative action. The Zapatista experience — 30+ years of patient autonomous institution-building — is the model, not the 1871 Paris Commune lasting 72 days.

**The Treaty as the site**: For Aotearoa, the Treaty relationship is the specific political terrain that all six thinkers must engage. Māori working-class people are subject to both colonial domination *and* class exploitation — and the two are not the same thing, as tino rangatiratanga scholars have consistently argued. A socialist politics for Aotearoa that does not centre the Treaty is, at best, incomplete, and, at worst, reproduces Pākehā domination in left-wing clothing. The pātaka must be built in partnership, on Treaty terms, not as a Pākehā socialist project that invites Māori to participate on its terms.

### 6.5 AI Warrior Closing Frame

The course title *Tangi o te Tāheke* — the sound of the waterfall — names what this series is for. The six thinkers together make a sound: a sound of things falling, being broken, being unmade. The poutama rises step by step. The kōwhaiwhai patterns loop and return.

For AI Warrior specifically:
- The course has built a toolkit for analysis: hegemony, mutual aid, debt, conscientisation, control societies, federation
- It has demonstrated that theory is practice by other means — every lesson was a study circle, a Freirean dialogue, a Zapatista *encuentro*
- The concluding task is not more theory — it is action: join, build, refuse, federate

Bakunin's last words in *Statism and Anarchy* (1873): "Freedom can be created only by freedom, that is, by a universal revolt of the people and free organization of the toiling masses from the bottom up." ([Anarchist Library](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/mikhail-bakunin-statism-and-anarchy))

This is the challenge. In Aotearoa 2025: from the bottom up, in partnership with tangata whenua, outside the algorithmic Leviathan, through the pātaka and the marae and the union hall and the study circle — *tāheke ki uta, awa ki tai*.
