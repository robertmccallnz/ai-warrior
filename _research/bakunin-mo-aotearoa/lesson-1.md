# Lesson 1 — Ko wai a Bakunin? The man Marx feared
*Tangi o te Tāheke — Bakunin mō Aotearoa · Lesson 1 of 6*

> "Freedom can be created only by freedom — that is, by a universal revolt of the people and the free organisation of the toiling masses from the bottom up."
> — Mikhail Bakunin, *Statism and Anarchy*, 1873

## The man Marx wrote in the margins about

In 1874 Karl Marx sat down with a copy of a Russian book and started writing notes in the margins. Not a review. Not a letter to a friend. A private, point-by-point demolition job, paragraph by paragraph, that he never published in his lifetime. The book was *Statism and Anarchy*. The author was Mikhail Bakunin — and the document Marx produced is now called *Conspectus of Bakunin's Statism and Anarchy* ([Marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1874/04/bakunin-notes.htm)).

You only do that for someone you take seriously. You only do that for someone whose argument is starting to land in places yours isn't.

So the first thing to know about Bakunin is this: by 1874, Marx — who two years earlier had run him out of the First International — was, privately, in his study, still arguing with him.

That's where we begin.

## A working chronology

Mikhail Alexandrovich Bakunin was born 30 May 1814 on his family's estate at Premukhino, in Tver province, Russia. He died 1 July 1876 in Berne, Switzerland, broke and unwell ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Bakunin)).

In between, he did about six lifetimes' worth of getting in trouble:

- **1842** — Publishes "The Reaction in Germany" in Arnold Ruge's *Deutsche Jahrbücher* under the pseudonym Jules Elysard. The essay ends with the line everyone misquotes — we'll come back to it in Lesson 3 ([Marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bakunin/works/1842/reaction-germany.htm)).
- **1848–49** — Fights in the Prague uprising and the Dresden insurrection. Yes, that Dresden — alongside the composer Richard Wagner, before Wagner became respectable.
- **1851–57** — Locked up in the Peter and Paul Fortress, then Schlüsselburg. Six years in tsarist cells. His teeth fall out from scurvy.
- **1857–61** — Internal exile in Siberia, from which he escapes the long way: across the Amur River to Japan, across the Pacific to San Francisco, across the United States, across the Atlantic to London. Three oceans to get back into the fight.
- **1864** — The First International is founded in London. Marx writes the inaugural address.
- **1868** — Bakunin joins the International. He also founds his own outfit inside it, the Alliance of Socialist Democracy. The Central Council is not amused.
- **1869** — Bakunin produces the first Russian translation of *The Communist Manifesto*. Read that twice: the man Marx is about to expel from the International is the one bringing Marx into Russian.
- **1871** — The Paris Commune. Both Marx and Bakunin defend it. Both draw opposite lessons from it — Lesson 4 of this course.
- **1872** — The Hague Congress. Bakunin and James Guillaume are expelled, 49 votes to 14, with 10 abstentions ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hague_Congress_(1872))).
- **1873** — Publishes *Statism and Anarchy*. The mature statement of his politics.
- **1876** — Dies in Berne, 1 July.
- **1882** — *God and the State*, his most-read book in English, is compiled from his manuscripts and published posthumously by Élisée Reclus and Carlo Cafiero.

## What the fight was actually about

The story you usually hear is that Marx and Bakunin had a personality clash. They did. Two strong men, two camps of loyalists, accusations of secret societies, even — absurdly — accusations of tsarist spying flying both ways.

But the personalities are not the story. The story is **organisational**.

By 1872 the Bakuninist federations of the International — the Spanish, the Italian, the Belgian, the Jurassian — were *growing faster than the Marx-aligned General Council in London* ([SDOnline](https://sdonline.org/issue/65/common-banner-marxists-and-anarchists-first-international)). Marx held the Hague Congress in the Hague specifically because it was a long way from the Bakuninist strongholds in Southern Europe, and he showed up with bloc-voted proxies.

The expulsion charge was secret-society membership. The actual disagreement was about four things:

1. **The form of the organisation.** Bakunin's Alliance was a network of loosely affiliated local groups with no central command. Marx's General Council wanted disciplined, centralised coordination.
2. **The question of the state.** Could socialists *use* state power as a transitional instrument toward liberation? Marx said yes. Bakunin said no — any seizure of state power would corrupt the movement, including a workers' seizure.
3. **Who fights.** Marx focused on the industrial proletariat. Bakunin thought the peasants and the *lumpen* — the people respectable Marxism wrote off — were just as revolutionary, sometimes more.
4. **Tempo.** Marx counted decades. Bakunin counted nights.

You can argue all four of those for the rest of your life. People have. What matters for our course is that **the disagreement is alive in 2026** — every time someone says "first we'll take state power, *then* we'll dismantle it," they are taking Marx's side. Every time someone says "the means *are* the end, you don't build freedom through a Leviathan," they are taking Bakunin's.

## Anarchist-collectivism, not what you think

Most people, if they have a picture of Bakunin at all, picture a bomb-thrower. That's wrong, and the wrongness is doing political work — it lets the modern reader file Bakunin under "interesting but unserious" and move on.

His actual position has a name. **Anarchist-collectivism**. Sometimes called **collectivist anarchism**. Here's what it means, and what it isn't:

- **Not anarcho-communism** (that's Kropotkin, Lesson 2 of *this* AI Warrior series): Kropotkin wants collective ownership *and* distribution according to need. Bakunin keeps a form of wage based on labour contributed within the collective.
- **Not anarcho-individualism** (Stirner, Tucker): Bakunin starts from solidarity, not from the sovereign individual.
- **Not mutualism** (Proudhon): Bakunin doesn't want exchange between small producers; he wants the means of production held *collectively*.

His positive programme:

- Means of production held in common by the workers who use them.
- Wages by contribution, inside the collective.
- Local communes federate voluntarily into regional bodies, regions into national or international bodies — **always from below, never by delegation downwards from above.**
- The state is not reformed. It is dissolved.

In his own words, from the writings collected by Maximoff (1953):

> "I mean that liberty of each which is not limited or restrained or curtailed by the liberty of another, but is strengthened and enlarged through it: the unlimited liberty of each through the liberty of all, liberty through solidarity, liberty in equality."
> ([Marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bakunin/works/writings/ch11.htm))

That sentence is the load-bearing one for our whole course. Read it twice. Liberty *through* solidarity. Liberty *in* equality. Not despite them. Not after them. *Through*.

## Why this matters in Aotearoa, June 2026

We are running this course for a specific reason. Across the AI Warrior thinkers series we have read Gramsci on hegemony, Kropotkin on mutual aid, Graeber on debt and bullshit jobs, Freire on the banking model of education, Deleuze on control societies. We are closing with Bakunin because he is the one who refuses to give us the clean exit.

Gramsci offers a long war of position inside institutions. Bakunin says: the institutions will eat you.

Kropotkin shows mutual aid as a fact of nature. Bakunin asks: at what scale can it federate?

Graeber shows us the cage of debt. Bakunin shows us the cage that *issues* the debt.

Freire teaches the oppressed to read the word and the world. Bakunin asks: and when you read it, what then?

Deleuze diagrams the rhizome. Bakunin says: walk it.

For Aotearoa, in 2026 — a small country whose state apparatus is in active retreat from Te Tiriti obligations, whose data is held by foreign platforms, whose AI policy is being written by the Crown without genuine Māori partnership — Bakunin's question is the question. **Where does power actually live, and what do we have to refuse to keep it living there?**

That's Lesson 2: **mana motuhake — federation from below**. We'll read Bakunin's free federation alongside He Whakaputanga 1835 and Te Tiriti 1840. We'll see resonances. We'll mark the limits. We will not collapse one into the other.

For now, sit with the chronology. Sit with the *Conspectus*. Sit with a man Marx could not stop arguing with, even after he had won the vote.

E kore e mau te rongo ki te whenua kāore e watea.
There is no peace on land that is not free.

## Whakaaro — discussion prompts

1. Marx's *Conspectus* is private margin-notes, never published in his lifetime. What does it tell us that he wrote it at all?
2. Bakunin translates the *Communist Manifesto* into Russian in 1869, three years before being expelled. What does this say about the nature of the split?
3. Which of the four disagreements at the Hague (form, the state, who fights, tempo) is most alive in Aotearoa politics today?
